## Planning for the assignment

The purpose is to demonstrate your understanding of the **process pattern** — a pattern that is descriptive, not instructional.

---

## Assignment topic

The goal is to research the best practices to get ready for getting the job you want when you graduate, then write up those best practices using the process pattern:

> Write a graduation learning plan to explain the best practices that you need to follow, so when you graduate having followed those best practices, you will have the skills needed to successfully apply for the job you picked in your compare and contrast assignment, the one you articulated in its recommendation.

While the intended audience is a Husky student just like you, a major takeaway of this assignment is to get you to consider what you can do beyond required coursework to increase your chances of landing your dream job after graduation. **The best indicator that you have written effective best practices is you take your own advice and try some or all of the recommended activities.** There will be an extra credit assignment later in the quarter that asks for a retrospective: are you yourself doing the activities you recommended? Why or why not?

## Basic specs

This section contains an overview of the assignment's basic specifications; see the rest of this document for more in-depth details.

- **Pattern:** process  
- **Delivery method:** A document in either Markdown (`.md`) or reStructuredText (`.rst`) format  
- **Primary audience:** a Husky student just like you (same degree plan, same specific job aspirations)

- **References:** at least one reference required

## Table of Contents (ToC)

The Table of Contents in modern document editors and formatting languages enable far more features than just providing the reader a high-level overview of the main contents of the document and their page number. When using formatting languages with features for browsers (like Markdown and reStructuredText), you have the ability to create links in the document, which not only take you to the document, but let you provide other readers a URL that will take them directly to that point in the document, saving valuable time in navigation.

Your table of contents should, at minimum:
- Provide meaningful descriptions for each section of your document
- Contain all headings at all levels
- Contain links

In order to turn a line in the ToC into a hyperlink, you follow this format: \[A Heading\]\(#a-heading\) with the ToC element in square brackets, and the hyperlink in parenthesis, all lowercase, and spaces replaced by hyphens. Look at this document's ToC in a raw text editor, such as an IDE like Visual Studio Code or Notepad, to understand the structure to do that.

## Headings

Each level of heading needs at least two headings. This is because a single heading disrupts flow and is better integrated into the greater section.

Markdown has multiple heading levels (usually at least 6), and all can be hyperlinked using the method provided in the [Table of Contents (TOC)](#table-of-contents-toc)

## Best Practices

Best practices differ from instructions or tutorials in that instructions or tutorials assume 0 pre-existing knowledge from the reader. Best practices guide the reader on the best way to perform a particular task, and why they should follow that process.

---

### Structure

Pages 77-79 of Edition 2 (Pages 7-9 in the PDF) provide examples for how to structure and write best practices.

---

### "Why"

Your primary audience for this assignment is unique in that they already have enough knowledge to use the tools and techniques. You do not need to reiterate how to use these tools, you need to guide them on the best way to use these tools, and **why** they should use it that way. Some tips:

- Avoid persuasive language or being overly instructive. Page 78 (Page 8 in the PDF) provides a concrete example, related to LinkedIn, as to how you should talk to your audience.

- Remember that your primary audience has around the same knowledge level as you. Ensure you talk to them as equals, 

- Avoid passive voice, which is different from the past tense. Passive voice usually happens swapping the subject and the object. Your reader has agency (the ability to make decisions and act independently). Write in a way that keeps your audience as the subject and tells them the action to take, instead of having what they act upon as the subject:
    - For example: "The table fell over" is passive voice. Who knocked the table over? You can change this active voice by writing "I knocked the table over."
    - The Passive vs Active voice worksheet will help you in avioding passive voice
    - With very few exceptions, you want to avoid passive voice in technical writing.

- Avoid if/thens or conditionals. As an example, if you say "If all you do is play games, then you won't learn how to make games."

---

### Command

The command is where you tell them what the audience should do:

- Provide specific examples of what to do and, if appropriate, examples of what the reader may have done or seen others do and why it is not good practice.
- While you are not required to provide examples of what someone might do that is not good practice, it is helpful for your readers to understand why following certain patterns do not provide as much benefit. For example:
    - For readers pursuing software development, while doing well in your core classes demonstrates a solid grounding in programming: 
        - Personal projects demonstrate to recruiters that you know how the challenges associated with building something real. 
        - Practicing leetcode problems will aid you in getting through the technical rounds of interviews.
    - For readers pursuing game development, while playing lots of games will help you find patterns of fun and elements that go into a good game:
        - Participating in Game Jams or designing games with friends demonstrates that you know the challenges associated with development
        - Running servers, or being a server adminstrator for games demonstrates your knowledge in what makes a game run behind-the-scenes.

## Figure/Visual Element

The visual element is highest-value element of the assignment. You will create a visual element that both supplements your document and conveys information more effectively than just text.

### Requirements

- Create using a tool (e.g., Canva) **or** directly in Word (Insert → Shapes)
- If borrowing data, include a **source line** under the image and in References.
- **You cannot borrow an entire figure, only images and content that help make up the figure**.

- Integrate the figure into the document (see textbook Chapter 12)
- Format the figure title so it:
- Appears **above** the image
- Has no spacing after
- Stays on the same page as the image

### Usefulness

- The visual element should **not** be a summary of the document's content, which does not add value.
- It should provide an explanation for something in a better format than pure text:
    - In scenarios where a figure is the best delivery method for the information, it is because it is easier for the **reader** to understand, instead of pure text which is usually easier for the writer to produce. 
        - The mistake here is often times the writer's description or explanation will result in something that is overly wordy or obtuse.
        - The writer will not effectively convey the concept, and the reader will discard the information.
    - A pie chart, for example, is an effective way to convey to the reader the highest value elements for a particluar set. 
    - With few exceptions, a table is not an acceptable figure. If you think you might have a table that's an exception, reach out to the instructor.

### References

While general-purpose AI tools (GPTs) can create citations, they frequently miss information or do not follow the citation style correctly. You should use a tool optimized for the task to get the most accurate citations:

- [Citation Machine](https://www.citationmachine.net/) is a web tool that will help you create citations and will produce correct citations every time.
- MS Word's References tool has the IEEE citation style built in, and is the recommended reference tool for this assignment. Entering the information for the source and formatting it to the citation style manually is error-prone. The citation tool takes in information for references using a form, and outputs a bibliography that follows every rule in the IEEE citation guide. [This article on Medium](https://medium.com/academicianhelp/ieee-referencing-using-microsoft-word-66c855181d64) shows you how to use the MS Word References tool to create perfect IEEE citations.

## Tone and Point of View (POV)

---

### Tone

- Write using a professional tone that assumes the reader is a fellow engineer.
- You are **not** providing a tutorial. 
- Assume the reader has enough experience to know how to complete the particular task. Your objective is to guide them on how to produce the **best** result for the task.
- Avoid 'talking down' (being overly explanatory or writing in a condescending tone) or 'talking up' (being overly formal as if you're talking to your boss). Your audience is a peer.

---

### POV

- Do **not** use 1st person POV, even though this is your plan  
- Do **not** use 3rd person POV to refer to the reader (e.g., “student”)  
- Use 3rd person POV **only** for people other than the reader
